HELP CONSERVE ANIMALS

There are currently 41,415 species on the endangerd animals list from all around the world.

This site provides information on how you can help protect them.

Bengal Tiger

Bengal tigers are the largest Asian big cat. They live in tropical rainforests and temperate forests and are an important part of their ecosystems. There are around 3,890 bengal tigers in the wild, and they are endangered because of poaching and habitat loss.

Find more information about tiger conservation at Panthera.org.
Giant Panda

Giant pandas have been driven from their habitats by bamboo forest deforestation. Pandas are vulnerable to habitat loss because 99% of their diet is bamboo.

Find more information about panda conservation from the WWF.
Elephant

Elephants are commonly poached for their tusks. Like many other species, they are threatened by habitat destruction.

Find more information about elephant conservation from the WWF.
Amur Leopard

Amur leopards are a leopard species that have adapted to survive in the temperate forests of northeastern China and far eastern Russia. One of the biggest threats to Amur Leopards is poaching. Amur Leopards are desired for their thick, spotted coat, and is very expensive. Also, their habitat is shrinking and their prey sources are depleted.

Find more information about lepord conservation at the African Wildlife Foundation.
Dolphins and Porpoises

Dolphins and porpoises are very similar to each other. They both are friendly and playful and are popular with wildlife watchers. Dolphins and Porpoises live in oceans and freshwater rivers. Many dolphins are critically endangered with population numbers under 100 animals. The Baiji is the first cetacean species to become extinct. They were also known as the Chinese River Dolphin

Find more information about dolphin and whale conservation from Porpoise.org.
Chipanzee

Groups of chimpanzees live together in forests and typically move around in trees but if on land they travel on all fours. Due to human interference, chimps have already disappeared from four different countries and are being threatened by poaching and disease.

Find more information about Chimpanzee conservation from the Chimphaven.
Gorilla

Gorillas are the largest primates in the world and travel in groups called troops. Although they do not have preditorial enemies, they are a victum of human habitat interference.

Find more information about Gorilla conservation at the International Gorilla Conservation Programme.
Vaquita

The most rare marine mammal in the world, Vaquita critically endangered having been the victim of dangerous equipment used for illegal fishing. There are only 30 currently living Vaquita left in the world.

Find more information about Vaquita conservation from www.vaquitacpr.org.
Plains Bison

Survivors of the Ice Age, Bison can weigh up to 2,000 pounds and run at 40 mph. Historically, Bison have been responsible for how land developed by determining growth patterns of vegetation. Due to human interference like the mass hunting in the 19th century there are only two pure public herds in america left.

Find more information about Bison conservation from the Wildlife Conservation Society.
Whale

There are currently in existence 86 separate species of whales that rome all over the world's oceans weighing between 20 and 200 tons! They are vital to protect because they are at the top of the food chain, controlling the population of hundreds of other wildlife and preventing them from over populating and changing the ecosystem.

Find more information about Whale conservation at the Whale and Dolphine Concervation.
Polar Bear

Polar bears inhabit the arctic sea and surrounding frozen land mass. They often spend 50% of the day hunting for food, mainly seals. Polar bears are important because they play a key role in the arctic ecosystem as the top of the food chain but are threatened by the warming climates and melting landscapes.

Find more information about Polar Bear conservation from Polar Bears International.
Bonobo

Bonobos are apes that live only around the Congo River in The Democratic Republic of Congo. They are a peaceful species and are lead by females of the pact. Due to factors like civil unrest, poverty, and the uncharted land of their habitat not much is know about the species other than they are one of humans closest living relatives.

Find more information about Bonobo conservation from the Bonobo Conservation Initiative.
